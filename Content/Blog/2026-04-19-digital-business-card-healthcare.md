Physicians in multi-specialty settings often carry multiple business cards — one for direct contact, another for department scheduling, a third for referrals. The result is fragmented contact information and a higher likelihood that patients reach the wrong number.

### The case for digital cards

Three fundamental limitations of paper:

**Multiple contact points.** A cardiologist needs to share not just their mobile, but the scheduling desk, ward clerk, pathology lab, and referral fax. A 3.5 × 2 inch card with one phone number was never designed for that.

**Hygiene concerns.** Research from Hannover Medical School documented bacterial persistence on hospital surfaces. Paper cards handled across clinical environments are an unnecessary contact vector — digital sharing eliminates it.

**Information currency.** Patient surveys show 80% prefer digital communication with healthcare providers, and 66% would choose a provider based on timely, consistent communication. Outdated contact info actively pushes patients away.

### What healthcare providers actually share

- Direct and department phone lines
- Referral coordinator email addresses
- Lab and imaging portal links
- After-hours contact options
- Complete address with floor/wing details
- Multiple language versions when appropriate

### Implementation

- **At hospitals:** NFC tags on badge lanyards — colleague-to-colleague contact sharing via phone tap, no app install required.
- **At conferences:** Network at scale; contacts are saved digitally and searchable.
- **For referrals:** Specialists can create context-specific cards with scheduling procedures, so referring physicians always have correct information.

### Sharing mechanisms

Two methods serve different contexts:

- **NFC tap** — close proximity, no shared surface
- **QR code** — distance, works for signage and reception

Neither requires the recipient to install anything.

### Privacy framework

Medical business cards maintain clear boundaries. Include credentials, contact details, practice information. Exclude patient data, diagnosis codes, insurance information. ### Card structure best practice

A comprehensive medical digital card contains:

- Name and relevant credentials (MD, FACC, etc.)
- Specialty and department affiliation
- Multiple phone numbers (direct, department, after-hours)
- Specialized email addresses
- Complete practice address
- Patient portal or booking links
- Professional headshot (optional)
- Academic profiles for research-focused physicians

**Strategic approach:** Create multiple card variants for different audiences — patient-focused versions with booking links, referral-specific versions with coordinator contact info, conference versions highlighting research interests.

### Why this matters

Healthcare environments never fit the single-contact-per-person model that paper business cards presume. Modern medical practice needs layered contact information that changes as staff rotates and practices evolve. Digital business cards that update instantly, eliminate physical contact, and consolidate multi-layered information are the practical evolution.
